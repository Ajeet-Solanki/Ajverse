The Case of the Cold Shower

The image above shows a pristine, modern bathroom with a standard electric water heater. A homeowner has just installed a high-efficiency, multi-function power shower head. They are thrilled.

However, after the very first shower, they have a complaint: "The shower starts perfectly hot, but after just 60 seconds, it goes completely ice cold! My heater is broken!"

The Question: What is the specific mechanical diagnosis for this symptom, and why did changing the shower head trigger it?

Hint 1: Stratification

Hot water naturally rises to the top, and cold water enters at the bottom. How does the heater ensure this stratification is maintained?

Hint 2: Flow Rates

Think about the one thing that changed. The new power shower head has a much higher flow rate. What fluid dynamic is now dominant?

The Diagnosis: Broken Dip Tube & Short Circuiting

The Dip Tube (which carries incoming cold water to the bottom of the tank) has likely cracked or broken near the top.

The Mechanism: Normally, cold water enters the bottom, pushing the hot water at the top out. If the dip tube is broken at the top, the cold water "short-circuits"—it enters the top of the tank and immediately mixes with the hot water being drawn out.

The Trigger: The old shower head had a low flow rate, allowing the water to stay somewhat stratified. The new **high-flow power head** creates higher turbulence, pulling that cold water straight into the shower line within 60 seconds!
